\begin{abstract}
Parkinson's Disease (PD) is a chronic neurodegenerative disorder. It consists several motor symptoms such as tremor, bradykinesia, postural instability, and freezing of gait (FoG). PD is currently diagnosed clinically through physical exam by health-care professionals , which can be time consuming and highly subjective. Wearable IMU sensors provides a promising gateway by allowing continuous monitoring of PD severity. 

We propose a Self-Supervised Bilateral Cross-Attention Encoder that processes wrist-worn IMU signals from the PADS dataset including 469 participants recordings containing three patient groups Parkinson's Disease(PD),Healthy Controls(HC) and Differential Diagnosis(DD). We break the detection task down into two focused binary classification tasks. The model learns two different objectives separately. 

The patient-level stratified 5-fold cross-validation, we have achieved a combined mean accuracy of $90.08$\% ($93.12$\% for HC~vs.~PD and $87.04$\% for PD~vs.~DD). The results emphasize the clinical challenge of distinguishing Parkinson's from other neurodegenerative diseases. Self-supervised representation learning using contrastive infoNCE loss gained  combined accuracy of 91.02\% using only 20\% of labeled data in a held-out fold. This demonstrates the effectiveness our method in transfer learning for clinical use with minimal labels.  The real-time applicability was tested by deploying the optimized model operating with a mean inference time of 48.32 ms per window on a Raspberry Pi CPU. This study can contribute in continuous monitoring of PD motor symptoms could help support timely clinical intervention and lessen the need for rare specialist visits.
\end{abstract}

Parkinson's Disease, IMU, wearable sensing, cross-attention transformer,
self-supervised learning, infoNCE, nested cross-validation, edge deployment
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

%the introduction section has been humanized. 
Parkinson's Disease (PD) is a progressive  neurodegenerative movement disorder that afflicts $10$~million individuals globally\citep{dorsey2018}. The disease is the result of the progressive degeneration of the dopaminergic system in the brain; Some leading symptoms of PD involves movement disorders, tremor, bradykinesia, rigidity postural instability and freezing of gait~(FoG). Parkinson’s disease (PD) can impact daily activities like walking, speech and other basic functioning.

As diseases progress, PD may lead to motor impairments as severe as balance dysfunction and dysphagia and cognitive deficits such as dementia and psychosis. although PD is uncurable, timely clinical intervention and regular check-ups can slow the disease's advancement and correlate with increased life-span ~\citep{bloem2021}.

Currently, PD diagnosis and monitoring requires active participation of healthcare professionals in addition to utilization of standard clinical scoring systems such as UNIFIED PARKINSON'S DISEASE RATING SCALE (UPDRS), Hoehn in Yard rating. These diagnostic approaches are subjective and constrained by inter-rater variability, and requires routined assessments. PD has overlapping motor symptoms with other neurological disorders which is group together as differential diagnoses~(DD): essential tremor, atypical Parkinsonism, multiple system atrophy, and progressive supranuclear palsy. DD can present motor patterns similar to PD, complicating diagnosis, especially at early stages and resulting in delayed treatment.
Only a limited number of studies focus on developing a system capable of detecting motor abnormalities and differentiating PD from differential diagnosis. The incorporation of wearable sensors into edge devices such as smartwatches provides a unique opportunity to assess the physiological and motor activity of patients in their natural environment without affecting their daily behavior [ 6, 7 ]. Wearable devices like smartwatches with embedded accelerators can capture the Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU) signals related to PD\@ motor symptoms.However, inferring meaningful patterns of such medical time series data is fundamentally challenging since noise and distortions disrupt from natural environmental variables. 
On the other hand, motor asymmetry is a well-known feature of PD and symptoms often onset and progress more severely on one side of body, by the application of bilateral smartwatches used on wrists this asymmetry may be captured without any clinical setup.


Recently, machine learning techniques have achieved promising results in Human Activity Recognition (HAR) tasks such as the PD recognition task. While sequential networks like LSTMs capture temporal dependencies in the signals generated from sensors, Transformer encoders with attention mechanism perform well when learning for patterns that have long-range temporal dependencies~\citep{vaswani2017}. Self-supervised contrastive representation learning is a powerful paradigm for learning from unlabelled data, which may be transferable to clinical environments where obtaining annotated data is limited by expert assessment and the cost of annotation. Nonetheless, real-time implementations of these systems are largely unexplored because of increased computation costs and the small amount of properly annotated data available in neurological diseases like PD which exhibit a high variability in patient symptoms.
In this study, we present a Self-Supervised Bilateral Cross-Attention framework for detecting Parkinson's disease using wrist-worn IMU data. The key contributions are:

\begin{itemize}

\item A bilateral cross-attention architecture that captures the motor asymmetry between left and right wrists, an established clinical feature of PD \@.

\item A hierarchical classification approach to decompose the three class classification problem into two binary classification subtasks (HCvs.PD and PDvs.DD) yielding $2.71$~pp combined accuracy gain over the three class classifier.

\item DHWT (Differential Hopping Windowing Technique), which establishes variable length overlapping throughout each individual class and relieves the source of underlying class imbalance from the dataset.

\item A self-supervised pre-training strategy for label efficient learning that achieves as close as $91.02\%$ combined accuracy with as few as $20\%$ labels.

\item Deploying the model on Raspberry Pi CPU and with minimum preprocessing attaining the inference rate of $48.32$~ms per window establishing feasibility of deployment on edge devices.

\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Generalizable Insights for Healthcare.}

The work yields three insights that generalize well beyond PD detection.
Firstly, Hierarchical binary decomposition beats multi-class first Many subproblems of a single classification, when clinically decision boundaries---a pattern that emerges whenever one has to diagnose a disease from healthy controls \emph{and} from phenotypically similar conditions. 
The second reason is that self-supervised pre-training with the raw IMU signals saturates quickly with little supervised data (${\sim}20\%$) than this suggests that the annotation bottleneck in wearable clinical studies can be significantly denoised by representation learning on unlabelled audio. 
In the third place, the cross-attention mechanism provides a general-purpose asymmetry that are derived from bilateral or contralateral asymmetry from Sensors and descriptors, wearables for any condition withogeneous between subjects. lateralised motor symptoms.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Objective of PD detection has been extensively investigated with wearable IMU sensors assessment.

Most of the earlier works relied on handcrafted features derived from accelerometer signals,to determine tremor and bradykinesia~\citep{patel2009}; recent work has focused on deep learning methods which showed competitive results without any manual feature engineering.~\cite{Jahanshahi et al. 2020}.

CNNs were used for IMU time series for gait and tremor classification~\citep{soumma2025fog}. While LSTMs have been applied to capture sequential motor signals dependencies~\citep{guo2022}, and CNN-Transformer hybrids for single waist-worn FoG detection have been employed accelerometer~\citep{sigcha2022}. A limitation common across this work is the heavy reliance on unilateral sensing,despite the well-known motor asymmetry in PD Symptoms.
Transformer architecture~\citep{vaswani2017} is suitable for modelling the irregular, periodic nature of motor symptoms, as self-attention lets the model make associations between distant timesteps simultaneously. 

For Human Activity Recognition (HAR) tasks, dual-stream architectures have been designed using parallel encoders for preprocessing various sensor modalities prior to fusion, The PD has also been explored in multi-scale frequency-aware architectures for severity assessment~\citep{zhao2025mfam}. Yet existing dual-stream designs fuse the streams using concatenation or addition, without any explicit modelling of the contralateral relation between left and right motor signals.


Self-supervised contrastive representation learning~\citep{oord2018} has become a powerful learning paradigm for unlabelled clinical data. TS-TCC~\citep{eldele2021} combines temporal and contextual dissimilarity. LIFT-PD~\citep{soumma2025liftpd} gaining competitive performance with $40\%$ fewer labelled samples. These approaches successfully transfer to downstream clinical task but have not been validated on bilateral IMU data for PD classification, where augmentation strategies need to ensure clinically meaningful asymmetric bands between the two wrist streams.


Finally, an continuing challenge in clinical ML is to avoid optimistic bias by conflating model selection with performance estimation~\citep{varma2006}. The vast majority of studies reporting PD detection evaluate results from a single split or flat $k$-fold; they yield models that are difficult to compare, and potentially too optimistic.
metrics.

All these challenges have been addressed in our studies, which addressed the challenges in detecting the asymmetry in PD symptoms, scarcity of labelled data, generalization and real-life deployability .

\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Overview}



%.....................................................

We use the PADS (Parkinson's Disease Smartwatch) dataset from
PhysioNet~\citep{varghese2024}. It contains bilateral wrist-worn smartwatch recordings from
469~participants performing 10~standardized motor tasks designed by expert neurologists. 
The dataset comprises three groups: 291~patients with PD, 79~Healthy Controls~(HC), and 99~patients with Differential Diagnosis~(DD), diseases that shows parkinson's like motor symptoms but are distinct neurological disorder including essential tremor, atypical Parkinsonism,secondary causes of Parkinsonism, and multiple sclerosis.
The dataset also includes demographics, medical history, and PD-specific non-motor symptom questionnaires, making it suitable for diagnostic studies.

Each participant wears two Apple Watch Series~4 devices, one on each wrist, providing six-axis IMU measurements: three accelerometer axes ($a_x, a_y, a_z$) capturing linear movement and three gyroscope axes ($\omega_x, \omega_y, \omega_z$) recording angular velocity. These tasks are \textit{CrossArms}, \textit{DrinkGlas}, \textit{Entrainment}, \textit{HoldWeight},\textit{LiftHold}, \textit{PointFinger}, \textit{Relaxed}, \textit{StretchHold}, \textit{TouchIndex}, and \textit{TouchNose}, Tasks \textit{Relaxed} and \textit{RelaxedTask} each being $10$~seconds to $20$~seconds long, sampled at $100$~Hz~frequency.

Figure~\ref{fig:sensor_viz} shows comparative data from randomly selected participants from HC,PD and DD classes performing the \textit{HoldWeight} task, showing distinct temporal patterns indicative of tremor and bradykinesia.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Problem Definition}


In all, each input sample is a pair of synchronous left $(L)$ and right $(R)$ wrist signals: 
$\mathbf{X} = (\mathbf{X}^L, \mathbf{X}^R) \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times 2C}$,  where $C=6$ is the number of sensor channels per wrist and $2C=12$ the total number of channels.  At $256$ time steps per window we have $T$. Each sample comes with a label  $(y \in \{\text{HC}, \text{PD}, \text{DD}\})$. 

We decompose classification into two binary tasks: the model learns 
$f : \mathbb{R}^{T \times 2C} \rightarrow [0, 1]^2$, outputing probabilities for  HC~vs.~PD and PD~vs.~DD. 

Raw signal $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{T_{\text{raw}} \times C}$ is transformed to $N$ windows after DHWT  (Section~\ref{sec:windowing}):  $\mathbf{X}_W = [\mathbf{x}_{w_1}, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_{w_N}] \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times T \times 2C}$, 
and a label is assigned for each window.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}

 The six channels of IMU signals per wrist, were selected, discarding any additional channels in the recordings. The first 0.5 seconds (50 samples) of each task is then cut-off to eliminate transient artifacts caused by sensor initialization and the participants settling into the assigned tasks.

\subsubsection{Down-Sampling and Band-pass filtering}
\label{sec:windowing}
 The signal was then down-sampled from 100 Hz to 64 Hz using polyphase resampling. This frequency in effective range for recognizing human motor activity with accelerometer sensors, despite reducing memory loads and computational complexity. 
Then, we applied a fifth-order Butterworth band-pass filter with a band of [0.1, 20] Hz using zero-phase forward-backward filtering (filtfilt) to avoid phase distortion. Most clinically relevant human motor activity locates below 20 Hz, such as resting tremor (4–6 Hz) and voluntary movement (< 5 Hz). The band-pass filtering preserves all crucial bands while cutting out noise such as low-frequency DC offsets and high-frequency sensor artifacts. 
The impact of this filtering step on model performance is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:ablation_bp}.

\subsubsection{Hierarchical Label encoding strategy}
\label{sec:masking} 
To design the hierarchical binary classification method, a masking strategy is applied during labelling. For a Healthy participant, the HC~vs.~PD label is set to $0$ while the PD~vs.~DD label is masked as $-1$; for a PD
participant, HC~vs.~PD head is lableled $1$ and PD~vs.~DD is labelled $0$; and for the DD
participant, HC~vs.~PD head is masked as $-1$ and PD~vs.~DD is $1$.
During training, the masked labels are excluded from loss calculation for the corresponding classification head. This ensures that each classifier is learning only the samples relevant to its decision boundary.  

 
\subsubsection{Imbalanced Data Mitigation}
\label{sec:windowing}
 
IMU signals are segmented into windows of $T{=}256$ timestamps
($4$~seconds at $64$~Hz). Uniform non-overlapping sampling produced severe class imbalance, due to highly disproportionate participant counts (HC:~79, PD:~291,DD:~99) in the dataset,causing the model to predict only majority class (PD). to mitigate this Differential Hopping Windowing Technique (DHWT)~\citep{soumma2025liftpd}  was applied . Which applies variable overlap per class: $70\%$ for HC, $0\%$ for PD, and $65\%$ for DD\@.
% The number of windows for a signal of length $L$ with window size $W$
% and step $s$ is:
% \begin{equation}
%   N_w = \left\lfloor \frac{L - W}{s} \right\rfloor + 1
% \end{equation}
This created around ${\sim}8{,}000$ windows per class(${\sim}25{,}640$ total), distributed evenly across folds.

A patient-level stratified 5-fold cross-validation is applied to form train set and validation set. Stratified cross-validation ensures equal distribution of class labels in every fold, for a less biased estimates of true out-of-sample performance. The patient-level splitting ensures patient-independent training of the model.
 


\subsection{Model Architecture and Training}
\label{sec:base_model}
The architecture employs parallel encoder streams to process both left and right wrist windows. that are connected by cross-attention , motivated by
the motor asymmetry of PD (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
Each wrist's $C{=}6$ channel input is linearly projected to
$d$~dimensions, and sinusoidal positional encodings are added accordingly ~\citep{vaswani2017}. There are N cross-attention layers, each consisting of three sub-layers: (i) cross-attention, where the embedding of one wrist queries that of the other wrist's values; (ii) self-attention on each stream independently; and (iii)~a position-wise feed-forward network (two-layer MLP, ReLU)
For the left stream:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{H}^L \leftarrow \mathrm{LayerNorm}\!\left(
      \mathbf{H}^L + \mathrm{MHA}(\mathbf{H}^L,\, \mathbf{H}^R,\,
      \mathbf{H}^R) \right)
\end{equation}
This produces $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{2d}$  by applying global average pooling over the output of the $N$ layers, giving $\mathbf{z}^L, \mathbf{z}^R \in \mathbb{R}^d$ which is then passed to two classification heads (linear + softmax).




Cross-entropy loss is calculated per head over non-masked samples, and the joint loss averages the two:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|} \sum_{s \in \mathcal{S}}
\mathcal{L}_s, \qquad
\mathcal{L}_s = -\sum_{c=1}^{2} y_{s,c} \log \hat{p}_{s,c}
\end{equation}
The AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019} (weight decay $0.01$) optimizer was used for training,
gradient clipping at unit norm, ReduceLROnPlateau (factor $0.5$,patience $5$), up to $100$ epochs with the batch size of~$32$ and a learning rate of $5{\times}10^{-4}$ and a  dropout~of $0.2$.
The embedding dimension of the base model is $d{=}64$, $N{=}3$
cross-attention layers, $h{=}8$ attention heads, feed-forward~$256$ dimensional feed-forward network layer.

\subsection{Self-Supervised Pre-training}
\label{sec:ssl}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1.0\textwidth,height=0.6\textheight,keepaspectratio]{cross_attn.png}
  \caption{Dual-channel cross-attention Transformer architecture.}
  \label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}


Self-supervised contrastive representation learning as a pre-training stage before fine-tuning on labelled data, to transfer useful structural information to downstream classification task.  
An anchor sample ~$z_i$, positive~$z_i^+$ augmented from the anchor sample, and negative samples~$z_j$ ($j \neq i$) which is augmented from different class than the anchor sample are constructed per batch.
Two augmentations technique alternate with $50\%$ probability: temporal warping~\citep{um2017} via cubic spline interpolation ($k{=}4$knots, $\mathcal{N}(1.0,\,0.3^2)$, pinned endpoints) and Gaussian
noise ($\sigma{=}0.05$). The infoNCE loss is calculated per batch with a random sampling strategy~\citep{oord2018} is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{NCE}} =
- \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B}
\log \frac{
\exp\!\left(\mathrm{sim}(z_i, z_i^+)/\tau\right)
}{
\sum_{j=1}^{B} \exp\!\left(\mathrm{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau\right)
}
\end{equation}
After pre-training, the full model is fine-tuned end-to-end.

All models were implemented in PyTorch and trained on a CUDA-compatible

\subsection{Self-Supervised Pre-training and Label Efficiency}

We achieved a performance of $93.56\%$ (HC~vs.~PD) and $92.50\%$ (PD~vs.~DD) for SSL model on $100\%$ of labels.
A combined accuracy of $93.03\%$ comparing with base model's result ($90.08\%$) beating the PD~vs.~DD thresholds, proving that self-supervised model learns better representations for hard boundary of the PD and DD classification.
The SSL representation reaches saturation with only $20\%$ of the labels, the model reaches $91.96\%$ combined accuracy; the improvement is within $\sim 1$~pp from $20\%$ to $100\%$ of the labels.
The linear probe evaluation with $100\%$ of labels, with frozen SSL encoder achieved $94.01\%$ overall accuracy ($94.12\%$ HC~vs.~PD, $93.90\%$ PD~vs.~DD), slightly exceeding the full fine-tuned model. This means that the contrastive pre-training learns representations which become linearly separable by a linear classifier provided sufficient labels, and that it is not strictly necessary to end-to-end fine-tune for strong performance with fully available labels. However, at lower fractions of labels the full fine-tuning has a noticeable contribution (e.g.\ $91.96\%$ vs.\ $82.14\%$ at $20\%$ labels), showing that strong performance can be achieved with as low as $20\%$ of data.

\subsection{Comparison with Foundation Model}
 
We fine-tuned the TimesFM~\citep{das2024}, a decoder-only foundation model
pre-trained on large-scale time series data, by prefixing with a LoRA
adapter~\citep{hu2022} (rank $r{=}6$, $\alpha{=}12$, dropout $0.15$,
regularisation $0.1$) to the hidden dimension of $1{,}280$.
Across 5-fold cross-validation, TimesFM+LoRA achieved
$93.62\%\pm0.06\%$~(HC~vs.~PD) and $92.89\%\pm0.39\%$~(PD~vs.~DD), for a
combined accuracy of $93.26\%$---with a high stability in standard deviation and
competitive with the best models from this study.

\subsection{Hyper-parameter Search using Nested-Cross Validation}
We also conducted a nested cross-validation experiment with Optuna (TPE sampler, 20~trials with 3~inner folds) to search hyper-parameters across outer fold, to train the selected configuration for
80~epochs. This yielded $93.61\%\pm0.09\%$~(HC~vs.~PD) and $92.57\%\pm0.07\%$~(PD~vs.~DD), with an average $93.09\%\pm0.05\%$ with, making it the most consistent near-zero variance result across folds. The Optuna search consistently favoured smaller architectures ($d{=}32$-$64$, $N{=}~2$--$3$ layers), confirming that smaller properly regularised model generalize well on this dataset.

\subsection{Edge Deployment}
\label{sec:pi}
We used the Optuna-optimized model ($d{=}32$, $N{=}3$) for evaluating the feasibility of real-time inference on Raspberry Pi~4 (ARM Cortex-A72, 4GB RAM). The pipeline reads from incoming samples at 100Hz, down-samples them to 64Hz and then applies a fifth order causal Butterworth band-pass filter one sample-by-sample, with the second-order sections (SOS) filter with a persistent filter state and $256$ samples are generated in a circular buffer.  Inference triggered every $128$ samples added to the buffer ($50\%$ overlap, $2.0$s step), resulting in predictions at $\sim 0.5$~Hz. The mean inference time of one sample is $48.87 \pm 4.52$~ms, throughput of $20.46$~FPS, $CPU$ usage: $0.2\%$, RAM consumption: $816.1$~MB.
This demonstrated that the model can be comfortably accommodated by the resource constraints of a current smartwatch processor.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The hierarchical binary classification strategy, performs better than the three-class baseline model by $2.71\%$ points in combined accuracy. The three-class model achieves near-perfect PD accuracy at $99.89\%$, but sacrifices HC and DD performance. This shows that focusing on maximizing accuracy for a single class tends to favor the easiest boundary. Focusing on one objective favors the easiest boundary, as each head specializes in the hierarchical approach, crucial for the clinically challenging PD~vs.~DD boundary. 

The breakdown by fold shows that PD~vs.~DD accuracy varies significantly, ranging from $82.33\%$ to $92.51\%$ with a standard deviation of $3.66\%$, likely due to the inherent complexity and variability in distinguishing between these conditions. HC vs. PD remains stable, with a standard deviation of $0.43\%$. This highlights the importance of cross-validated report rather than just averages, which could hide variability.

Band-pass filtering, which isolates the motor-relevant signal within the $0.1$ to $20$~Hz band, provides a $7.14\%$ point improvement in HC~vs.~PD accuracy and reduces variance between folds by six times. This confirms that the motor-relevant signal lies within the $0.1$ to $20$~Hz band, while out-of-band noise disrupts learning. 

SSL pre-training shows strong label efficiency, achieving $91.02\%$ accuracy with $20\%$ of labels. Beyond this point, returns diminish. The linear probe, reaching $93.32\%$ accuracy with full labels, confirms that SSL features capture general motor patterns and can gain good performance with as little training as training the classifier only. In clinical settings where specialist annotation is a bottleneck, this suggests that the burden of annotation can be greatly reduced.

The comparison reveals that the base model, SSL fine-tuning, and TimesFm fine-tuning all achieved around $93\%$ combined accuracy on the same partition, suggesting that these models effectively leverage pre-trained temporal representations for clinical IMU classification. The foundation model's competitive accuracy indicates that pre-trained temporal representations transfer well to clinical IMU classification, while the base model has a smaller deployment footprint.

Edge deployment shows a response time of $48.32$~ms and $0.02\%$ CPU usage, confirming that real-time monitoring is feasible on limited hardware. 

The task-wise CNN and BiLSTM ablation reveals that no single baseline architecture is superior across all motor tasks. Tasks involving sustained postures, like HoldWeight and LiftHold, benefit from the temporal modeling of the BiLSTM. In contrast, tasks with more localized spatial patterns favor the CNN. Notably, several tasks, such as Entrainment, PointFinger, TouchIndex, and TouchNose, remain below $70\%$ accuracy for both architectures. This suggests that these motor tasks produce patterns that are genuinely hard to distinguish at the window level. This drives the design of the cross-attention Transformer, which collects information from both sides across all timesteps instead of relying on a single bias.



\subsection{Limitations}
Experiments are performed using a single dataset (PADS). Test on an independent cohort is necessary to confirm generalization. 
The patient partition must be maintained across all training. SSL pretraining is done on the same dataset which was used for fine-tuning. Pre-training would ideally exploit a larger corpus of unlabeled IMU data from many different populations. Clinical verification by experts against true diagnosis has not been conducted and is essential. The Raspberry Pi results emulate smartwatch deployment but do not directly measure power consumption on target hardware.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ═════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Here we have proposed a Dual-Channel Cross-Attention Encoder for PD detection from bilateral wrist-worn IMU data. Through cross-attention, the model captures motor asymmetry.

a hierarchical binary classification approach which divides into focused HC\~vs.\~PD and PD\~vs.\~DD tasks, improving over three-class baseline achieved 93.12\% HC\~vs.\~PD and 87.04\% PD\~vs.\~DD accuracy, where cross-validation showed that primary challenge of distinguishing the PD\~vs.\~DD boundary.

SSL Pre-Training Brings 91.02\% Combined Accuracy With 20\% Labels, and a pre-trained foundation model (TimesFM+LoRA) attains similar performance, validating that self-supervised representation learning are effective approaches for this clinical setting. The main limitations include dependency on one dataset. 

Future work should, explore task-aware fusion for challenging motor tasks, and extend to longitudinal data.

for disease progression monitoring. Eventually, a validated continuous monitoring system may relieve specialist clinics and provide earlier intervention for millions of patients living with Parkinson’s Disease.
